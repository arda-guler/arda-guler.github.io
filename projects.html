Simulators

Simulations are only as good as our understanding of the universe is. They are often even worse due to the limitations of the tools we use to simulate (now computers, quantum computers and supercomputers). However, most of the time, they are more than good enough and they allow us to analyze systems in fast, cheap and flexible ways.


OrbitSim3D is an orbital/celestial mechanics and space mission simulator. It uses n-body physics, which is a more realistic representation of the real universe than the Keplerian 2-body systems are. It can also account for the perturbations from the non-spherical gravity sources. Using OS3D you can simulate moon systems, planetary systems, star systems, lagrange points, asteroid passes, deep space maneuvers, geostationary satellites, Hohmann transfers and many other things you can imagine. It is highly customizable and you can create just about any system you like, as long as relativistic effects can be ignored. It can calculate orbital parameters, plot variables over time, or display variables in real-time. It is also quite simple and only uses a few thousand lines of code. There is no 3rd party physics engine involved, and rendering is done in an extremely simple way via OpenGL.

OrbitSim3D is licensed under MIT License.

Supercluster3D is a cosmic scale n-body simulation that is also meant to account for various equations modeling the expansion of the universe. It can randomly generate and simulate hundreds (or many thousands, if someone has lots of time and/or a good computer) of galaxies in volumes of space that are usually measured using Megaparsecs.

Supercluster3D was originally created as a response to the floating point precision errors I was facing in OrbitSim3D with extremely large and small numbers. I built the n-body simulation from ground up, but this time, I gave up on the SI units such as meters or seconds and tried to keep all numbers close to 1, by using units like Megaparsecs (and more arbitrary ones like 1E40 kilograms) in my computations. Originally I wanted to code Supercluster3D in Python just like OS3D, but simulating the interactions between hundreds of galaxies turned out to be extraordinarily slow. I thought that I finally hit the limit where I'd have to switch to a compiled language (and a fast one at that), and rewrote the program in C++. Using the GLFW library, I also implemented a basic 3D visualization.

Supercluster3D is licensed under MIT License.

TrajectorySim is a sounding rocket simulator. I programmed it specifically for the student rocketry society I am a part of - METU Rocket Society. Basic equations governing the flight of a rocket are actually pretty simple, but what is so impressive about this simulator is that it can do complex drag calculations. It is incredibly hard to get drag equations right without real data, and this is about as good as you can get without CFD or wind tunnel tests.

Mechuilibria is a 2D particle mass simulator. It can be used to evaluate simple static or dynamic systems. You can create links with various stiffness values, apply forces, calculate the center of mass of systems and observe their movements.

I started working on Mechuilibria as a double pendulum calculator (after seeing some interesting videos on YouTube), but as I finished programming the pendulum script, I saw that this simple physics engine had a much bigger potential. Now, you can not only create pendulums, but also cars, cranes, struts, trusses or whatever other 2D systems you can come up with.

Mechuilibria is licensed under MIT License.

Bussard Ramjet is exactly about the concept it is named after: the interstellar spacecraft propulsion system. It is a rather simplistic Python project that tries to simulate an interstellar spaceship scooping hydrogen from the (now better understood) interstellar medium and using the proton-proton chain to generate thrust using its nuclear fusion reactor.

Bussard Ramjet is licensed under MIT License.

Science and Engineering Tools

Apart from simulators, there are some simpler, often specialized calculation or helper tools that I've written.


Varieye is basically a FITS file reader for data from NASA space telescopes such as Kepler or TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite). I've written this tool for my own use while I was examining the "Planet Hunters" citizen-science project on zooniverse.org. It aids the eye by doing data binning and representing the general shape of the light curves with a red line.

Varieye is licensed under MIT License.

RMS Arm visualizes the kinematics of a 3 segment robotic arm with shoulder, elbow and wrist joints with limited angle movement. Although it is not a true recreation, it was inspired by the Canadarm aboard ISS and the earlier ones that flew on the US Space Shuttle. It uses OpenGL for 3D rendering.

RMS stands for Remote Manipulator System.

RMS Arm is licensed under MIT License.

Injector Calc is a flow calculator tool for the initial portion of a symmetrical impinging injector setup under various acceleration levels.

As much as it is a useful calculator, it was also a nice practice for GUI design. (However the GUI library I've been using had major changes since its beta version and it now relies on an earlier version of the said library.)

The calculations of the rest of the flow sequence is partly chaotic and requires CFD (or a flow test) - this is just a helper tool to give a quick idea to the user.

Music and Art Software

I also program stuff that can help you get inspired.


pyTuneGen is an automated music generator written in Python. (It can export them as MIDIs too!) It can optionally be customized by picking scales, time signatures, bpms; or with some rather unorthodox variables (e.g. to control what ratio of bars you want to be repeated).

You may use this as a tool to get inspired -I sometimes do that myself-, or you can pick the good portions of a particular tune to use in your music. (No, I won't sue you.) That being said, there is no artificial intelligence involved; so if you are a music creator, it might also be a good tool to clean up your plagiarist subconscious.

If you've been doing that for long enough, you know what I'm talking about.

pyTuneGen is available on PyPI and can easily be installed by the command 'pip install pytunegen'.

pyTuneGen is licensed under MIT License.

This repository contains a bunch of simple image processing tools, which I use to give certain effects or apply filters to, well, images!
There are tools that can apply various types of contrast, which I usually use to give images a retro 8-color display effect. This collection of tools could actually be merged into a single program with various modes, but I think just double-clicking on what you want makes everything faster. Disagree? It is open source, and the MIT License is pretty relaxed, take the scripts and do whatever you want!

Image tools are licensed under MIT License.

ASCII Randfield is a random ASCII art generator. It combines shapes such as lines, rectangles, discs etc. and generates an ASCII image. You may let it run with no parameters or configure it according to your liking. It's mainly meant to be an inspiration tool to create landscapes, cityscapes, abstract art or whatever you can make of it. Its "stream" feature can also be used to generate glitchy graphic effects.

ASCII Randfield repository also includes seven_lines.py, which is a "command line screensaver".

ASCII Randfield is licensed under MIT License.

Games

Games? Games! Is there anything else to say?


miniDocking3D is a space station docking simulation game inspired by SpaceX ISS Docking Simulator. However, there are distinct differences. I'm using OpenGL wireframe graphics for rendering, the rotation is already locked into docking orientation, and there is an autopilot! Also mine has sound too, it beeps and wails alarms when necessary. You can also hear the nice puffing sounds of the RCS thrusters.

Ah, and since I thought it was too easy as is, you get no numbers on the HUD. Just shapes and guidelines.

Also the RCS thrusters sometimes do defective maneuvers. Haha :D

miniDocking3D is licensed under MIT License.

miniLanding3D is a lunar landing simulation game. You take manual control (with optional autothrottle autopilot) of a lunar lander at the final phase of the landing, and you have to cancel your velocity and make a soft touchdown before you run out of propellant.

ML3D was partially inspired by Eagle Lander 3D, a more complex simulator which simulates the historic Apollo lunar landings. My graphics might not compare, but hey - the physics and the control mechanisms are the same! (Also the code is shorter.)

miniLanding3D is licensed under MIT License.

HaritaWars is a mini strategy game based on a similar game I used to play in high school with my friends. The digital version gives the control of countries to AI players (either with predefined actions or machine learning algorithms) and allows the user to watch the events unfold as AIs do their decisions. The game runs until there is one country remaining - it might take 30 turns, or it might take 3000.

HaritaWars also comes with a map creation tool and instructions on how to interface your AI algorithm with the game.

HaritaWars is licensed under MIT License.

Soundscape Matrices is an audio-based navigation game. The only visual guide you can get is the map of the place you are in - and that is only available in the easy difficulty! You'll have to explore the place and guide yourself by using distinct sounds that indicate walls, doors, keys and evil spirits who are eager to haunt you.

...oh yeah, the game has a slightly heavy atmosphere. I didn't mention that, did I?

Soundscape Matrics is licensed under MIT License.
